Will other feature-selection methods find better DMLs, including "weak" ones?
This is a legitimate and good instinct—DSS adjusted-p + delta rank is a univariate, marginal ranking, and it is well known that marginally weak features can be jointly informative. Reasonable alternatives, in rough order of how much I'd trust them on your sample size:
Stability selection (Meinshausen & Bühlmann) layered on top of the elastic net is the single best fit for your situation. You subsample the training data many times, fit the penalized model each time, and keep loci selected at high frequency. It directly addresses your point 4 (which DMLs are real vs. noise) and your feature-selection question simultaneously, and it is built to control false discoveries. I'd prioritize this.
Sparse PLS-DA is designed for p≫n omics classification and can capture multivariate weak signal that univariate ranking misses.
A simple multivariate filter before the model (e.g., keep DSS-significant DMLs, then let elastic net do selection within them) is essentially what you already do; the upgrade is to let selection be data-driven and stability-assessed rather than a fixed top-K cut.
Tree ensembles (random forest / gradient boosting) with permutation importance can surface interactions, but on n≈54 they tend to overfit and I would use them only as a sensitivity check, not the primary model.
A caution that applies to all of these: every method you try is a researcher degree of freedom. If you run five feature-selection pipelines and report the best, you have re-introduced the multiplicity problem at the meta level. Decide the primary method in advance (I'd nominate stability selection + elastic net), and report the others only as clearly-labeled sensitivity analyses with their honest, not cherry-picked, numbers.
